## Required article structure

Most blog drafts should include:

1. Title and meta description
2. TL;DR near the top
3. Clear answer to the search intent in the opening
4. Main article body
5. Internal links to relevant sections/pages on the same site
6. External sources linked contextually on the claims they support
7. FAQ section near the end
8. Suggested CTA aligned with the site's conversion goal
9. Suggested feature image brief using this site's image style guide

## TL;DR section

Put a short TL;DR near the beginning.

Good TL;DR:

- 3 to 5 bullets
- answers the query quickly
- mentions the recommended next step
- avoids hype and vague claims

## Content capsule technique

Use content capsules for roughly 60 to 70 percent of the article.

A content capsule is a short, self-contained section that answers one specific sub-question or search-intent fragment. Each capsule should usually include:

1. A clear subheading
2. A direct answer in the first one or two sentences
3. Supporting detail, source, example, or data
4. A practical implication or next step
5. A relevant internal link where useful

Use capsules for:

- definitions
- comparisons
- steps
- common objections
- local/service nuances
- tool recommendations
- examples
- mistakes to avoid
- mini case studies

Do not turn the whole article into disconnected snippets. Use normal narrative sections where the reader needs context, flow, or a deeper explanation.

## Internal linking rules

Every draft should recommend internal links.

Use links to:

- relevant service pages
- product or feature pages
- related blog posts
- location pages
- resource pages
- pricing/contact/booking pages when naturally relevant

Rules:

- Use descriptive anchor text, not "click here".
- Link to the most contextually relevant page, not just the homepage.
- Prefer links that help the reader take the next step.
- Recommend 2 to 5 internal links for most blog posts.
- If the site has a hub/spoke structure, link up to the hub and across to related spokes.
- Include suggested up-links from existing pages to the new draft when possible.

## External source and citation rules

Back up factual claims with high quality sources.

Use external links for:

- statistics
- legal, medical, finance, or safety claims
- platform documentation
- technical claims
- research findings
- market/news claims
- definitions where an official source exists

Rules:

- Link the contextual keyword or claim, not a generic "source" label.
- Use original sources where possible: official docs, government pages, academic papers, primary research, reputable industry reports.
- Avoid low-quality scraped blogs, thin affiliate articles, or unsourced claims.
- Do not dump all links in a generic Sources section unless the user asks for a bibliography.
- If a claim cannot be sourced, soften it or remove it.

## FAQ section

Add an FAQ section near the end when the topic supports it.

FAQ rules:

- 3 to 6 questions is usually enough.
- Use real search intent, customer objections, or People Also Ask style questions.
- Give concise, useful answers.
- Do not repeat the article word for word.
- Include internal links where they genuinely help.

## Voice and style

The writing should match this client, not the template author's brand.

Default style:

- clear
- practical
- specific
- low hype
- useful to the target customer
- natural enough that it does not sound like a generic AI article

Avoid:

- filler intros
- unsupported claims
- overpromising
- generic AI phrases
- keyword stuffing
- em dashes in final copy
- title case headings unless the site style uses them

## Image guidance

Before generating feature images, create a website-specific image style guide.

Feature images should be consistent across the site. The guide should define:

- aspect ratio
- color palette
- mood
- illustration/photo/abstract style
- recurring shapes or visual motifs
- text policy, if any
- negative prompts
- examples to match
- examples to avoid

Do not reuse another brand's visual style unless the user explicitly wants that style for their site.
